Option A — "n·" ★ Recommended

A typographic lettermark: a bold lowercase "n" with an amber dot to its upper right. The dot is the captured moment — small, bright, impossible to miss. The purple-to-near-black gradient background is distinctly brand-coded without needing any camera imagery.

  • Most distinctive of the four — no competing camera app uses a lettermark like this. VSCO uses "vsco", BeReal uses "BeReal" text, none use a symbol+dot combo.
  • Scales perfectly to 20pt (notification icon) — the "n" + amber dot remain legible where film frames and shutters become unreadable noise.
  • Extendable to all brand materials: loading screen, splash, app clip icon, widget background, share sheet thumbnail. The "n·" mark becomes a visual signature.
  • The amber light leak in the top-right corner ties the icon to the in-app film aesthetic without being literal about it.

Option B — "Moment Frame" Strong runner-up

A film frame in portrait orientation — sprocket holes at top and bottom, warm amber glow inside the frame suggesting a scene yet to be developed. The most literal translation of the film roll metaphor.

  • Immediately communicates "film photography" — the sprocket perforations are unmistakably analog. Works well for the Huji/Dispo-aware Gen Z user who recognises film aesthetics.
  • The amber interior glow suggests a moment being "held" inside the frame — poetic and on-brand.
  • Risk: similar territory to Huji, Dispo, and some VSCO visual language. Less distinctive as a logo mark compared to Option A.
  • At 60pt the sprocket holes can read as noise. Works better at 96pt+ where the frame structure is clear.

Option C — "Shutter" Contextual pick

The matte physical shutter button from the Capture Hub, scaled to fill the icon. The object that users tap hundreds of times becomes the face of the app — familiarity as identity.

  • Strong brand continuity — users see the same object in the icon as the one they tap inside the app. Creates an immediate "I know what this does" moment on the home screen.
  • The matte cream gradient reads as premium and physical — distinctly different from the flat/glassy icons surrounding it on any home screen.
  • Risk: at 60pt the concentric circles can look generic (too similar to a generic "camera" or "record" button icon). The film-specific personality is less obvious.
  • Best use case: as a secondary icon option (Settings → App Icon) for users who prefer a more minimal/abstract mark.

Option D — "Golden Hour" Alt / seasonal icon

The icon IS a photograph — a golden-hour sky rendered as a film strip with sprocket perforations along both edges. No UI chrome, no lettermark. Pure atmosphere. The icon says "this is what your memories feel like."

  • Most emotionally resonant — it immediately communicates the app's core promise (beautiful, atmospheric moments) rather than its mechanism (film/camera).
  • Works brilliantly as a seasonal or limited alternate icon: different gradients for different times of year (golden hour, blue hour, midnight, winter light).
  • Risk: reads as "photo gallery" or "weather app" rather than "camera app" to a new user. Weak discoverability in the App Store where icon clarity matters.
  • Best use: as an alternate icon (the Amalfi Golden Hour edition) that power users can select. Creates a premium "I found the secret icon" moment.
★ Primary recommendation

Go with "n·" — Option A

The lettermark is the strongest long-term brand asset. At every size from 1024pt (App Store hero) down to 20pt (notification badge), the "n" + amber dot reads instantly and unambiguously. No camera app in the Gen Z space owns this type of mark.

The amber dot — the "·" in "n·" — is the whole product concept in one glyph: a single moment, bright, precise, captured. It scales into a design language: use it as a bullet point in marketing copy, as a loading indicator, as the "new capture" badge on the widget.

For engineering
The final icon should be exported as a vector path (not live text) for the "n" glyph, using the exact stem/curve geometry of SF Pro Display Black or Helvetica Neue Bold as the reference — converted to outlines in Figma/Sketch before handing off to Xcode.
Alternate icons (post-MVP)
Offer Option D "Golden Hour" as a selectable alternate icon (Settings → Appearance → App Icon). Different gradient variants per preset — AMALFI golden, NORDIC blue, TOKYO neon — gives power users a personalization moment.
Dark mode / tinted icons
iOS 18+ supports automatic icon tinting. The "n·" icon adapts gracefully: the purple-black bg accepts system tint naturally; the amber dot acts as an accent that resists tinting (set it as a non-tintable layer).
App Clip / widget icon
The amber dot alone (just the circle, no "n") works as a micro-icon at 16–20pt in widgets and App Clip banners. It's immediately brand-coded once users have seen the full icon a few times.
